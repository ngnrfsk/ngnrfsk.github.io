## City of London NO2 Trends: A Data-Driven Analysis

Nitrogen dioxide (NO2) pollution has been declining across London since 2017, driven by cleaner vehicle fleets, the Ultra Low Emission Zone (ULEZ), and broader policy interventions. The City of London presents a particularly interesting case study, combining high traffic volumes with a concentrated commercial area and evolving transport patterns.

In this post, we examine NO2 trends using diffusion tube monitoring data spanning 2017-2025, with statistical projections extending to 2030. The interactive map below demonstrates how effective visualization can communicate complex temporal and spatial air quality data.

### Background: NO2 and Health

NO2 is primarily emitted by road traffic, particularly diesel vehicles. Exposure to elevated NO2 concentrations is associated with:

- Respiratory symptoms and reduced lung function
- Increased susceptibility to respiratory infections
- Exacerbation of asthma and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)
- Cardiovascular effects in long-term exposure

**UK Legal Limit**: 40 µg/m³ (annual mean)
**WHO Guideline**: 10 µg/m³ (annual mean, updated 2021)

While the UK legal limit focuses on protecting public health from acute effects, the WHO guideline reflects growing evidence of health impacts at lower concentrations.

### The City of London Context

The City of London (the "Square Mile") presents unique air quality challenges:

- **High Traffic Density**: Major arterial routes and congestion
- **Urban Canyon Effects**: Tall buildings restrict pollutant dispersion
- **Commuter Patterns**: Daytime population far exceeds residential population
- **Fleet Composition**: Historically high diesel vehicle proportions (taxis, buses, delivery vehicles)

However, several factors have driven improvements:

- **ULEZ Expansion**: Tighter vehicle emission standards since 2019
- **Fleet Renewal**: Retirement of older diesel vehicles, uptake of hybrid/electric vehicles
- **Covid-19 Impacts**: Reduced traffic during 2020-2021
- **Active Travel**: Increased cycling and pedestrian infrastructure

### Monitoring Approach

This analysis uses data from a network of **diffusion tube** monitoring sites across the City of London. Diffusion tubes:

- Provide cost-effective spatial coverage
- Measure monthly average NO2 concentrations
- Are positioned at locations of public exposure (roadside, building facades)
- Require laboratory analysis and bias adjustment to ensure accuracy

The monitoring network captures pollution variability across different street configurations, traffic volumes, and building heights.

### Key Findings

#### 1. Sustained Decline Since 2017

Across the monitoring network, NO2 concentrations have declined substantially:

- **2017 Baseline**: Many sites exceeded 40 µg/m³ (UK legal limit)
- **2025 Status**: Widespread compliance with legal limits achieved
- **Average Reduction**: Approximately 30-40% decline network-wide
- **Rate of Change**: Steepest reductions 2017-2020, continued gradual improvement thereafter

#### 2. Spatial Variability Persists

Despite overall improvements, notable spatial patterns remain:

- **Hotspots**: Sites on major arterial routes (e.g., Upper Thames Street, London Wall) show elevated levels
- **Urban Canyons**: Street configurations affecting dispersion create localized higher concentrations
- **Background Sites**: Areas away from major roads approach WHO guideline levels

This variability highlights the importance of spatial monitoring networks in capturing exposure diversity.

#### 3. Projection to 2030

Statistical trend analysis projects continued gradual improvement:

- **2030 Forecast**: Further 15-20% reduction anticipated
- **WHO Guideline**: Most sites likely to remain above 10 µg/m³ WHO guideline
- **Uncertainty**: Projections assume continued policy pressure and fleet renewal

**Caveats**: Projections are based on historical trends and do not account for potential disruptions (policy changes, economic shocks, technology shifts). They should be interpreted as "business as usual" scenarios rather than definitive predictions.

### Policy Implications

The City of London trends demonstrate:

1. **Policy Effectiveness**: ULEZ and fleet renewal strategies have delivered measurable air quality improvements
2. **Ongoing Challenges**: Achieving WHO guideline levels requires sustained action beyond current policies
3. **Targeted Interventions**: Remaining hotspots indicate need for localized traffic management
4. **Monitoring Value**: Long-term diffusion tube networks provide essential evidence for assessing policy effectiveness
